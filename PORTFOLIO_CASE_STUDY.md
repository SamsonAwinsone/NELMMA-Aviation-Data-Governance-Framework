## 4. Portfolio Case Study Summary

### The Problem
Nelmma Aviation, a 20-year-old Ghanaian aviation and logistics operator, had a data governance framework that existed only on paper. Policies were inactive, roles were undefined, no one was trained beyond initial onboarding, and executive leadership was disengaged from compliance decisions. As the company expanded into EU and China markets — each carrying their own regulatory requirements — the governance vacuum became a strategic liability.

### The Process
Our team conducted a full governance assessment using structured interviews with the executive team, focus groups with operational staff, and document review of existing (inactive) policies. We benchmarked Nelmma's current state against DAMA-DMBOK and ISO standards to quantify the maturity gap.

We then designed the framework in three layers:
1. **People Layer** — Governance hierarchy, role definitions, RACI matrix, decision rights
2. **Process Layer** — Policy suite (8 documents), lifecycle management, audit workflows, escalation paths
3. **Technology Layer** — RBAC framework, data lineage architecture, compliance dashboard requirements

### The Output
- 8 standalone data governance policies
- Governance Charter formally establishing the program
- Phased 6-year implementation roadmap
- Complete RACI matrix
- KPI framework with measurable targets
- Multi-jurisdictional compliance mapping
- Stakeholder engagement summary
- Board-ready presentation deck

### The Impact
If implemented, the framework directly mitigates regulatory exposure under Act 843 and GDPR, enables the airline's GSSA partnership strategy, and creates the governance infrastructure necessary for GCAA scheduled operator certification — the company's primary strategic objective.

### Key Takeaway
This project demonstrated that data governance design is as much a human and organizational challenge as a technical one. The most valuable work was not the policy documents — it was the stakeholder alignment, the role clarity, and the executive buy-in strategy.

## 5. Interview Talking Points

**Q: Tell me about a data governance project you've worked on.**

*"I led the governance framework design for Nelmma Aviation, a Ghanaian aviation and logistics company. The engagement began with a maturity assessment — we identified 5 critical failure areas including undefined data ownership, inactive policies, and no compliance monitoring. The challenge was that policies existed but were completely unenforced. So the real work was designing governance around people and process before technology. We produced a full framework aligned to DAMA-DMBOK and ISO 38505, an 8-policy suite covering everything from data classification to breach response, and a 6-year implementation roadmap. I learned that governance frameworks fail when they're technical documents instead of organizational tools — the RACI matrix and role definitions were more valuable than any individual policy."*

---

**Q: How did you approach stakeholder analysis?**

*"We actually experienced two rejections before reaching Nelmma — one company refused due to data sensitivity concerns, another had too rigid a structure for us to work with. That selection process itself taught me a lot about governance in practice: organizations are often guarded about their data practices. With Nelmma, we conducted structured interviews with executives and focus groups with operational staff. The biggest insight was the gap between what leadership believed was happening with data and what staff actually experienced. That informed how we structured the governance hierarchy — specifically the escalation paths, which needed to be practical enough that staff would actually use them."*

---

**Q: How do you handle multi-jurisdictional compliance?**

*"Nelmma operates across 5 markets — Ghana, Nigeria, Liberia, Belgium, and China — each with different regulatory requirements. Our approach was to build the framework around the most stringent applicable standard and then layer in jurisdiction-specific requirements. Ghana's Act 843 was the baseline; GDPR applied to Belgian operations and any EU data subjects; PIPL applied to the China corridor. We mapped every policy to its applicable regulations and built a cross-border data transfer policy specifically for the Ghana-to-EU and Ghana-to-China corridors. The key lesson is that compliance isn't a checkbox — it's a continuous alignment exercise that must be built into the governance lifecycle."*

---

**Q: What would you do differently if you started this project again?**

*"I would prioritize the data catalog and metadata standards earlier in the process. We designed the governance structure and policies first, which was right — but without a shared understanding of what data assets actually existed, some of our role assignments and quality targets were defined in the abstract. A metadata-first approach would have grounded the framework in Nelmma's actual data landscape from the start. I'd also push harder for executive participation in the assessment phase — we got good data from operational staff, but executive-level buy-in came later than ideal."*
